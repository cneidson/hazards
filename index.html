Storm Risk and Environmental Hazards in NYC

As a densely populated coastal city, New York City is highly vulnerable to coastal storms, including nor'easters, tropical storms and hurricanes.

Residents living in flood-prone areas face risks not only from coastal storms themselves, but also from long-term environmental and public health hazards. Much of this is linked to the long history of manufacturing and industrial enterprises on the city's waterfront.

Navigating flood and storm risk

As part of its disaster management planning, the City of New York has designated six hurricane evacuation zones to identify areas likely to be inundated by storm surge. In the event of a hurricane or tropical storm, residents in the hurricane zones may be ordered to evacuate.

There are six levels of evacuation zones. Zone 1 has the highest risk and is the most likely to flood.

Storms and economic inequality

For many New Yorkers, the city's storm vulnerability was made frighteningly apparent when Hurricane Sandy hit on October 29, 2012. The storm resulted in 44 deaths, and an estimated $19 billion in economic loses. More than 69,000 residential units were damaged, and thousands of residents were temporarily displaced.

One of the communities hardest hit by the storm was the Rockaways, the area that sits on a barrier beach off the southeast end of Queens. Even before Sandy, the Rockaways suffered from deep income inequalities. A quarter of the Far Rockaway population (and one-third of all children on the peninsula) live below the federal poverty level. Re-development of the area post-Sandy has led to further economic disparities.

Flooding and industrial hazards

The City of New York has made numerous efforts to protect its working waterfront, including the designation of seven Significant Maritime and Industrial Areas (SMIAs).

SMIAs are designed to protect and encourage concentrated use of the waterfront for industrial firms and water-dependent businesses. However, because of the industrial nature of these areas, the SMIAs are home to facilities that use, store, or transport hazardous or toxic substances at higher concentrations.


(Image credit: City Limits)

All of the SMIAs are located within hurricane evacuation zones. This means the risk of accidental or uncontrolled release of toxic substances due to flood or storm surge is heightened. This represents significant environmental and public health risks for the surrounding communities.

Next, we'll explore some of the unique vulnerabilities of these areas, based on research from New York City Environmental Justice Alliance.


(Images credits: The New York Times)

Newtown Creek: A federal superfund site

Newtown Creek is a 3.5-mile tributary of the East River that forms part of the border between the boroughs of Brooklyn and Queens. The area is known for its long industrial history, as well as the Greenpoint Oil Spill, one of the largest oil spills ever recorded in the United States. More than 17 million gallons of petroleum (more than twice the amount of crude oil that spilled from the Exxon Valdez) leaked into the soil of the creekbed and surrrounding area over the course of nearly three decades.


(Image credit: Newtown Creek Alliance)

Today, the entirety of Newtown Creek is a federal superfund site, and its banks also include state superfund and brownfield sites, and numerous businesses that use industrial solvents containing trichloroethylene, a known carcinogen. During Hurricane Sandy, residents reported standing water with noxious odor and an oily sheen. The contaminated water penetrated businesses and homes, complicating cleanup efforts.

South Bronx: High-capacity chemical and oil storage

New York state regulates and monitors faciliities that store petroleum, hazardous substances/chemicals or liquefied natural gas at high capacities. Improper handling of these substances represents siginificant public safety hazards including fires and chemical burns, as well as long-term health risks from exposure to toxic substances.


(Image credit: Untapped Cities)

There are 171 of these facilities in New York City, but 11% are clustered within a half-mile of highly flood-vulnerable South Bronx SMIA. Accidential spills of toxic materials at these facilities can easily spread to nearby wells, water bodies, and homes.

In addition to the SMIA, the area is also surrounded by numerous highways, fossil fuel power plants, waste transfer stations, and diesel truck-intensive facilities. Because of the high intensity of industrial use, residents of the area have no recreational access to the waterfront at all.

Sunset Park SMIA: TRI Sites

The Sunset Park SMIA is home to 17 sites listed on the U.S. EPA's Toxic Release Inventory (TRI). TRI tracks the management of certain toxic chemicals that may pose a threat to human health and the environment.

Frequent and extensive flooding from coastal storms like hurricanes may increase the risk of spills at chemical facilities near waterways. Federal law does not explicitly require TRI sites to take extra precautions against flooding even if they are located in floodprone areas.


(Image credit: Untapped Cities)

Industrial flooding during Sandy: Red Hook and Gowanus

When Hurricane Sandy hit the southwest Brooklyn neighborhoods of Red Hook and Gowanus, the dangers of flooding and storm surge were accompanied by fears of long-term health effects from contaiminated in the water.

In addition to the Red Hook SMIA, the area is home to the Gowanus Canal, a Federal superfund site considered to be one of the most contaiminated bodies of water in the country, as well as state brownfields, and an aging and over-burdened sewer system. Residents forced to wade through the water brought in by Sandy reported "a greasy, oily slick" that clung to their skin.






(Images credits: New York Daily News, Brownstoner, The Real Deal)

Who is at risk?

In addition to the immediate risks from storm surge, coastal storms increase the risk of environmental hazards which translate into negative health effects, loss of wages, and housing instability. For these reasons, low-income residents are likely to bear the greatest burdens of environmental hazards.


(Images credit: Curbed New York)

All seven SMIAs are located in predominantly low-income, communities of color.
